## HR / Growth Mindset Questions

### Tell me about a time you were wrong about something technical and how you responded.
Central to Microsoft's growth-mindset framing — genuine intellectual humility matters more than a
polished "I was never really wrong" answer.

### Describe a time you learned a completely new skill or technology under time pressure.
Show a real learning process, not just the outcome — Microsoft explicitly values continuous
learning as a cultural pillar.

### Tell me about a time you gave or received difficult feedback.
Cover both directions if possible — Microsoft's collaborative culture expects comfort with
feedback in both roles.

### Why do you want to work at Microsoft?
Connect to something specific — Azure's growth trajectory, a product you're excited about, or the
growth-mindset culture itself — avoid generic "big impact" framing.

### Describe a situation where you had to collaborate with someone very different from you in
working style.
Reflects Microsoft's emphasis on collaborative, not competitive, teamwork.

### Tell me about a project that didn't go as planned. What did you do?
Be honest about the setback and focus on what you did next and what you learned.

### How do you approach mentoring others or being mentored?
Even early-career candidates are asked this — growth-mindset culture values two-way learning
relationships.

### Describe a time you had to advocate for a technical decision you believed in.
Show reasoned conviction balanced with openness to being convinced otherwise.

### What does "growth mindset" mean to you, practically, in your day-to-day work?
No single scripted answer expected — a thoughtful, genuine response about learning from failure
and embracing challenges works best.

### How do you stay current with new technologies?
Mention specific habits — courses, personal projects, following Azure/GitHub release notes, etc.

### Do you have any questions for us?
Ask something specific about the team's current technical challenges or how they apply growth
mindset in practice — generic questions read poorly.
